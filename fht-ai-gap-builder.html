Fiduciary Oversight
Three core questions — sufficient for a complete analysis.
3 core questions
Core questions
Has the plan's investment advisor signed a named fiduciary agreement — a 3(21) co-fiduciary acknowledgement or 3(38) investment management agreement?
Why this matters: Most bank and wirehouse advisors have never signed one. Without it, 100% of investment liability stays with the committee personally.
Does the committee have signed meeting minutes from each of the last four quarters?
Why this matters: Signed minutes are the primary evidence of a prudent process. Without them the committee cannot defend any investment decision it has made.
Have all committee members completed documented fiduciary training in the last 12 months?
Why this matters: ERISA holds committee members to the prudent expert standard. Annual training with written acknowledgement is the documented evidence each member is working to meet it.

Does the committee have a formal written charter defining its membership, responsibilities, quorum, and meeting schedule?
Why this matters: A committee charter defines who has fiduciary authority and how it is exercised. Without it the committee's legal structure is informal and potentially ambiguous.
Has the committee conducted a formal due diligence review of all service providers in the last 24 months?
Why this matters: ERISA requires ongoing monitoring of service providers, not just initial selection. A documented review demonstrates active oversight.
Is there a process for identifying and managing conflicts of interest among committee members and service providers?
Why this matters: ERISA's prohibited transaction rules require disclosure and management of conflicts. An undisclosed conflict can void an otherwise prudent decision.

Administration & Operations
Four core questions covering the most common DOL audit triggers.
4 core questions
Core questions
Was the most recent Form 5500 filed on time with no late or amended filings?
Why this matters: A late or amended 5500 is the #1 trigger for DOL audit selection. Verify on DOL EFAST2 — do not rely on verbal confirmation.
Is there a fidelity bond covering at least 10% of plan assets currently in force?
Why this matters: Legally required under ERISA Section 412. Operating without one is a current statutory violation. Request the certificate of insurance — not verbal confirmation.
Has a formal, documented fee benchmarking study been completed in the last 24 months?
Why this matters: ERISA's duty of loyalty requires all fees to be reasonable. Without a documented study the committee has no defense against an excessive fee claim.
Is the plan's all-in cost estimated above 0.75% of assets based on the 408(b)(2) disclosure?
Why this matters: The peer median for plans $10M–$100M is approximately 0.62%. Above 0.75% creates demonstrable excessive fee exposure with quantifiable participant harm.

Additional — optional
Has the plan document been restated within the last 6 years with all amendments adopted timely?
Why this matters: An outdated plan document can disqualify the plan and result in significant tax liability for participants and the employer.
Has a current Summary Plan Description been distributed to all participants within the required timeframe?
Why this matters: Distributing the SPD is ERISA-mandated. Failure to distribute creates participant claims exposure and DOL penalties.
Has annual ADP/ACP discrimination testing been completed on time with corrective distributions made within the required deadline?
Why this matters: Missed testing deadlines result in mandatory excise taxes and can disqualify the plan.
Have all revenue sharing arrangements been reviewed, documented, and either justified or eliminated?
Why this matters: Undisclosed revenue sharing is one of the most common elements of ERISA excessive compensation claims.

Investment Oversight
Two core questions identify the most common investment process failures.
2 core questions
Core questions
Is there a current, signed Investment Policy Statement on file?
Why this matters: The IPS is the foundational document of a prudent investment process. Without one, every fund decision is legally indefensible — there is no written standard to evaluate it against.
Has the fund lineup been formally reviewed and benchmarked against peers in the last 12 months?
Why this matters: ERISA requires ongoing monitoring — not just initial fund selection. Documented quarterly reviews are the evidence the committee is actively exercising prudent oversight.
Additional questions
— 3 more for deeper coverage
Additional — optional
Is there a formally designated QDIA meeting DOL safe harbor requirements with proper participant notice?
Why this matters: Without proper QDIA designation and notice, the committee retains liability for all participants who default into the plan without making an investment election.
Has the fund lineup been reviewed for lower-cost institutional share class alternatives?
Why this matters: Failure to use the lowest-cost available share class is a common element of ERISA excessive fee lawsuits.
Is there a formal watch list process for underperforming funds with documented criteria for replacement?
Why this matters: Without documented watch list criteria, the committee has no framework for demonstrating active, prudent management of the investment menu.

Participant Outcomes
Three core questions identify the plan design gaps that most directly harm retirement readiness.
3 core questions
Core questions
Is the plan's current participation rate below 80%?
Why this matters: 80% is the ERISA best-practice threshold. Low participation is almost always a plan design problem — correctable through auto-enrollment.
Is the employer match absent or below the industry peer median of approximately 50% of the first 6% of salary?
Why this matters: A below-median match suppresses participation and deferral rates and creates a recruiting disadvantage.
Is auto-enrollment inactive or set at a default deferral rate below 6%?
Why this matters: Plans with auto-enrollment at 6% consistently show 15–25% higher participation. Without it, improvement relies entirely on voluntary employee action.

Additional — optional
Is auto-escalation in place — automatically increasing deferrals by 1% annually up to a 10% cap?
Why this matters: Most participants set a deferral rate at enrollment and never increase it. Auto-escalation is the most effective mechanism for improving retirement readiness beyond initial enrollment.
Does the plan provide an annual financial wellness program with personalized retirement readiness reporting?
Why this matters: Participants who understand their retirement readiness are significantly more likely to increase their deferral rate.
Are pre-retirement counseling and distribution options clearly communicated to participants approaching retirement age?
Why this matters: Poor distribution counseling results in suboptimal tax decisions and is an increasing source of participant complaints and litigation.

Technology & Cybersecurity
One core question covers the most universal cybersecurity gap.
1 core question
Core question
Does the plan lack a written cybersecurity policy meeting the DOL's April 2021 guidance?
Why this matters: The DOL's 2021 guidance created specific compliance obligations for all ERISA plans. This is the most commonly unaddressed gap in plans under $100M.
Additional questions
— 3 more for deeper coverage
Additional — optional
Is multi-factor authentication required for all participant and administrator access to plan accounts?
Why this matters: MFA is the single most effective control against credential-based account theft and is explicitly required under the DOL's April 2021 guidance.
Has the recordkeeper provided a current cybersecurity attestation letter within the last 12 months?
Why this matters: ERISA fiduciaries are responsible for the cybersecurity practices of their service providers. A current attestation is the primary evidence of ongoing monitoring.
Does the plan have a documented business continuity and disaster recovery plan covering plan data and participant account access?
Why this matters: DOL guidance requires fiduciaries to assess and document business continuity arrangements for all critical service providers.
